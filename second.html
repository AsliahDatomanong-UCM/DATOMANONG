Origins in the Qur'an

Ramadan (Ramadhan), the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar, holds profound significance for over 1.8 billion Muslims worldwide. Its roots trace back to 610 CE when the Qur'an — Islam's holy book — was first revealed to Prophet Muhammad by the angel Gabriel (Jibril). This divine event transformed Ramadan into the "month of revelation," making fasting (sawm) one of Islam's Five Pillars.

The First Revelation: Cave of Hira

In 610 CE, during Ramadan, 40-year-old Muhammad retreated to the Cave of Hira on Jabal al-Nour (Mountain of Light) near Mecca for meditation. Amid pre-Islamic Arabia's polytheistic society, Gabriel appeared and commanded:

"Recite in the name of your Lord who created..." (Qur'an, Surah Al-Alaq 96:1-5)

This moment, Laylat al-Qadr (Night of Power), initiated the Qur'an's revelation over 23 years.

Establishment of Fasting

Fasting became a pillar of Islam in 624 CE, two years after the Hijra to Medina. Initially voluntary, it was formalized via Qur'anic verse 2:183, emphasizing piety and self-restraint. Early Muslims fasted from dawn to sunset, adapting pre-Islamic customs into spiritual discipline.

Key Historical Events

  • Battle of Badr (624 CE): Muslims fasted despite hunger, crediting divine aid for victory.
  • Conquest of Mecca (630 CE): Ramadan fasting unified tribes for peaceful surrender.
  • Ottoman and Abbasid Eras: Grand iftars and taraweeh prayers spread cultural traditions.
